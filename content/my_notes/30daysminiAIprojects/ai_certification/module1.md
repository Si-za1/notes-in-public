Module 1

To power these capabilities, 
AI learns from data and follows specific rules. 
When we combine the training and the rules, we get what's called an AI model. 

Some of the most powerful AI models 
are called foundation models, or FMs. They are programs 
that are trained on incredibly large data sets 
with generalized and unlabeled data. 
Typically, such data comes from publicly available sources over the internet.


Because of generalized knowledge, foundation models can complete a wide variety of general purpose tasks. 
Pretty much anything from having natural conversations 
to creating visuals and automating long and complex processes.


Developing and training a foundation model 
could be very expensive. But the good news is that many pre-trained FMs are publicly available as is  or you can customize them for your business needs.


At AWS, you can access FMs
and privately fine-tune them 
with your local data in Amazon Bedrock. 
These FMs can specialize in one task or be multi-modal, 
meaning they can handle different types of requests. 
Some create images 
while others can work with text, images, and videos.

Amazon Nova models, for example, 
can quickly analyze documents and videos, 
understand charts and diagrams, 
generate engaging content, 
and help you build sophisticated AI agents.

Models that specialize in language-related tasks 
are called large language models, LLMs. 
They can write stories, answer questions, 
translate languages, and write code. 
You might already know some LLMs 
like Amazon Titan Text and Claude. 
To interact with these models, 
you can enter a prompt in a chat window. 
For example, you might say, 
"Imagine you are an AI career coach. 
Provide guidance on what makes a resume stand out."

During training, a language model learns by studying content 
from books, articles, and websites. 
The model breaks the content 
into smaller pieces called tokens. 
This might be words, sub-words, and characters. 
Then the model turns tokens into numbers 
that are called embeddings. 
Embeddings represent tokens numerically 
and help the model understand the relationship 
between tokens and their meaning.


## Core dimensions of responsible AI:

- Fairness
    
- Explainability
    
- Privacy and security
    
- Safety
    
- Controllability
    
- Veracity and robustness
    
- Governance
    
- Transparency


A prompt is the question, request, 
or statement that you're making to the AI. 
That's it. 
It could be incredibly simple, 
such as check the spelling of my document, 
or it could be really complex. 
Something like write a Python function 
that trains a CCN and have these inputs and output. 
Yeah, right? The requirement list goes on. 
You're gonna have to learn how to adjust 
or engineer the prompts 
that you're feeding into your AI models. 

The goal is that your AI does what you want 
or gives you the output that you need. 
Now, that might sound simple, 
but crafting a well-structured prompt is a skill in itself.
